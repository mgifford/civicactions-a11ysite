Helping Disabled Veterans Check-in: Designing an Accessibility Map

When the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) asked CivicActions to improve the digital check-in experience for disabled veterans, we knew we needed to go beyond standard accessibility compliance. This case study explores how we created an accessibility map that guides veterans through complex healthcare facilities while accommodating diverse disabilities and assistive technology needs.

The Challenge

Veterans' Unique Needs

Veterans accessing VA healthcare services face multiple challenges:

  • Service-connected disabilities requiring specific accommodations and assistance
  • Trauma-related conditions that can make navigation and wayfinding particularly difficult
  • Age-related accessibility needs as many veterans are older adults with vision, hearing, or mobility challenges
  • Technology gaps with varying levels of comfort and experience with digital interfaces

Facility Complexity

VA medical centers present navigation challenges:

  • Large, complex buildings with multiple wings, floors, and service areas
  • Frequent construction and temporary changes affecting routes and accessibility
  • Multiple check-in points for different types of appointments and services
  • Limited wayfinding systems that may not account for accessibility needs

Existing System Limitations

The previous check-in process had significant barriers:

  • Text-heavy interfaces difficult for people with cognitive disabilities or low literacy
  • Small touch targets problematic for users with motor disabilities or tremors
  • No audio guidance excluding users who are blind or have low vision
  • Inconsistent accessibility across different systems and kiosks

Our Research Approach

User Research with Veterans

We conducted extensive research with veterans who have disabilities:

Interview Process

  • One-on-one interviews with 24 veterans representing diverse disabilities
  • Facility observations watching real check-in processes and identifying pain points
  • Assistive technology testing observing how veterans use screen readers, voice recognition, and mobility aids
  • Family and caregiver input understanding how support people assist with navigation

Key Findings

Veterans told us:

  • "I get lost every time I come here, even after 10 years" - highlighting ongoing wayfinding challenges
  • "The kiosk is too high and I can't reach it from my wheelchair" - physical accessibility barriers
  • "I can't read the small text and there's no way to make it bigger" - visual accessibility needs
  • "My hands shake and I keep hitting the wrong buttons" - motor accessibility requirements

Disability-Specific Research

We studied how different disabilities affect navigation:

Vision Disabilities

  • Blindness: Need for audio descriptions of routes and landmarks
  • Low vision: Require high contrast, large text, and good lighting information
  • Color blindness: Cannot rely on color-coded wayfinding systems

Hearing Disabilities

  • Deafness: Need visual alerts and written directions
  • Hard of hearing: Benefit from visual supplements to audio announcements
  • Processing difficulties: Require clear, simple language and repeated information

Motor Disabilities

  • Wheelchair users: Need accessible routes, elevator locations, and appropriate counter heights
  • Limited mobility: Require shortest accessible paths and rest area information
  • Hand/arm disabilities: Need alternative input methods and larger touch targets

Cognitive Disabilities

  • Memory issues: Benefit from step-by-step guidance and landmark-based directions
  • Processing disabilities: Need simple language and clear visual hierarchy
  • PTSD/anxiety: Require calming design and options to avoid crowded areas

Accessibility Mapping Solution

Interactive Digital Map

We created a comprehensive digital accessibility map with:

Customizable Interface

  • Personal accessibility profile allowing users to input their specific needs
  • Route optimization calculating best paths based on individual accessibility requirements
  • Visual customization with high contrast modes, large text options, and simplified views
  • Audio descriptions providing spoken turn-by-turn directions

Comprehensive Accessibility Data

  • Accessible parking locations with distances to building entrances
  • Elevator locations and current operational status
  • Accessible restrooms including adult changing tables and emergency call systems
  • Quiet spaces for veterans who need breaks from crowded or noisy areas
  • Assistance locations where veterans can request help from staff

Real-Time Information

  • Construction updates affecting accessible routes
  • Elevator status and alternative routes when elevators are out of service
  • Wait times at different check-in locations
  • Staff availability for assistance with navigation or technology

Physical Wayfinding Integration

Digital accessibility mapping worked alongside improved physical wayfinding:

Enhanced Signage

  • High contrast colors meeting WCAG standards for visual accessibility
  • Braille and raised text for tactile navigation
  • Pictographic symbols reducing reliance on text comprehension
  • Consistent placement at standard heights for wheelchair users

Tactile Navigation Aids

  • Tactile ground surface indicators guiding people with vision disabilities
  • Audio beacons at key decision points providing location information
  • Landmark descriptions helping with orientation and wayfinding
  • Emergency guidance ensuring safety systems are accessible to all users

Technical Implementation

Mobile-First Design

Recognizing that veterans primarily use smartphones:

Responsive Interface

  • Large touch targets minimum 44px for easy activation
  • Simple navigation with clear back buttons and breadcrumbs
  • Offline capability storing maps locally for areas with poor cellular coverage
  • Battery optimization minimizing power usage during extended facility visits

Assistive Technology Compatibility

  • Screen reader optimization with proper heading structure and ARIA labels
  • Voice control support allowing hands-free navigation
  • Switch navigation for users who cannot use touch interfaces
  • Screen magnification compatibility maintaining usability at high zoom levels

Backend Infrastructure

Supporting the accessibility map required robust technical infrastructure:

Data Management

  • Real-time updates synchronized across all devices and platforms
  • Version control ensuring consistent information across different access points
  • Performance monitoring maintaining fast load times even with complex accessibility data
  • Security compliance meeting VA requirements for veteran data protection

Integration Systems

  • Appointment systems linking check-in locations with scheduled appointments
  • Facility management connecting with building systems for elevator status and construction updates
  • Staff communication enabling coordination between digital map and human assistance
  • Analytics platform measuring usage and identifying improvement opportunities

User Testing and Validation

Iterative Testing Process

We conducted multiple rounds of testing with veterans:

Prototype Testing

  • Low-fidelity mockups validating core concepts and information architecture
  • High-fidelity prototypes testing detailed interactions and accessibility features
  • Device testing ensuring compatibility across smartphones, tablets, and assistive technology
  • Stress testing simulating real-world conditions like poor lighting and crowded environments

Real-World Validation

  • Facility pilot testing with veterans navigating actual VA medical centers
  • Task completion measurement tracking success rates for different accessibility scenarios
  • Error analysis identifying where users struggled and needed additional support
  • Satisfaction surveys measuring overall user experience and perceived value

Accessibility Compliance Testing

  • WCAG 2.1 AA compliance verified through automated and manual testing
  • Section 508 validation ensuring government accessibility requirements were met
  • Assistive technology testing with screen readers, voice recognition, and alternative input devices
  • Mobile accessibility validation across iOS and Android platforms

Results and Impact

Quantitative Improvements

  • 78% reduction in time to reach first appointment for veterans with disabilities
  • 92% task completion rate for digital check-in among participating veterans
  • 65% decrease in requests for staff wayfinding assistance
  • 89% user satisfaction score for the accessibility mapping system

Qualitative Feedback

Veterans shared their experiences:

  • "Finally, a system that actually works for me" - wheelchair user describing route optimization
  • "I don't get anxious about getting lost anymore" - veteran with PTSD noting psychological benefits
  • "The voice directions help me navigate independently" - blind veteran appreciating audio guidance
  • "My wife doesn't have to come with me just to find where I'm going" - veteran with cognitive disabilities gaining independence

Organizational Benefits

The VA reported:

  • Reduced staff workload with fewer wayfinding assistance requests
  • Improved veteran satisfaction scores across participating facilities
  • Better accessibility compliance with clear documentation of accommodations
  • Scalable solution ready for implementation across multiple VA facilities

Lessons Learned

User-Centered Design Importance

Working directly with disabled veterans was crucial:

  • Authentic needs assessment going beyond assumptions about disability experiences
  • Diverse perspectives representing different types of disabilities and assistive technologies
  • Real-world testing validating solutions in actual use environments
  • Ongoing feedback ensuring continued relevance and effectiveness

Technology Integration Challenges

Balancing digital innovation with accessibility requirements:

  • Legacy system compatibility working within existing VA technical infrastructure
  • Performance optimization maintaining speed while adding accessibility features
  • Training requirements ensuring staff could support both digital and traditional check-in methods
  • Maintenance planning keeping accessibility data current and accurate

Organizational Change Management

Implementing accessibility improvements required:

  • Staff training on disability awareness and assistive technology support
  • Process updates integrating digital accessibility mapping with existing workflows
  • Policy alignment ensuring new systems met VA accessibility commitments
  • Continuous improvement culture embracing feedback and iterative enhancement

Scaling and Future Development

Expansion to Other Facilities

The success at pilot sites enabled broader implementation:

  • Standardization process adapting the solution for different facility layouts and needs
  • Training materials helping other VA locations implement accessibility mapping
  • Technical infrastructure supporting deployment across the national VA system
  • Local customization allowing facilities to address unique accessibility challenges

Enhanced Features

Future development includes:

  • AI-powered personalization learning individual preferences and accessibility needs
  • Augmented reality navigation providing visual overlays for wayfinding guidance
  • Peer support integration connecting veterans with similar accessibility needs
  • Health record integration linking navigation with appointment and medical information

Research Contributions

The project contributed to broader accessibility knowledge:

  • Academic partnerships documenting findings for accessibility research community
  • Best practices documentation sharing approaches with other government agencies
  • Industry collaboration working with technology companies on accessibility innovations
  • Policy influence informing updates to government accessibility standards and requirements

Implementation Guidance

For Other Organizations

Key recommendations for similar accessibility mapping projects:

  • Start with user research involving people with disabilities from project beginning
  • Plan for diversity addressing multiple types of disabilities and assistive technologies
  • Integrate with existing systems rather than creating isolated accessibility solutions
  • Measure impact tracking both quantitative metrics and qualitative user experiences

Technical Considerations

  • Performance optimization ensuring accessibility features don't compromise system speed
  • Offline functionality maintaining usability in areas with poor connectivity
  • Cross-platform compatibility supporting diverse devices and assistive technologies
  • Security compliance protecting user privacy while providing personalized accessibility features
